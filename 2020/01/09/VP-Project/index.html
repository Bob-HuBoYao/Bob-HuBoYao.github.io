An improved view registration method and its application in laser imaging measurements

Boyao Hu1 and Lin Ma2
1Saint Paul’s School, Concord, NH 03301, USA
2Department of Mechanical and Aerospace Engineering, University of Virginia, Charlottesville,
VA 22903, USA

        View registration (VR) is a procedure that determines the orientations and locations in a given coordinate system based on images captured by one or more cameras. Existing VR procedures typically rely on a set of images captured on a fixed calibration target (referred to as FVM, fixed view method, hereafter). This work reports the development and application of a new view registration procedure, referred to as the variable view method (VVM). The VVM procedure relies on a set of images taken on a calibration target at various orientations and/or distances. As shown in this work, both numerically and experimentally, with a set of carefully selected views, the VVM can significantly improve the VR accuracy compared to the FVM. Such enhanced accuracy is expected to benefit a range of applications, and its potential to improve the accuracy of common laser imaging measurements are discussed.

I. Introduction

        View registration (VR) is a procedure that determines the orientations and locations in a given coordinate system based on images captured by one or more cameras [1-3]. The VR procedure is essential for many imaging applications [1, 4, 5], and this work was performed under the context of flow and combustion imaging diagnostics, such as PIV (particle imaging velocimetry), PLIF (planar laser induced fluorescence), and 3D tomographic measurements [1, 4, 5]. Due to its wide range of applications, significant research has been invested to develop VR procedures in the past [2, 3, 6-10]. Established VR procedures typically rely on a set of images captured on a fixed calibration target (referred to as FVM, fixed view method, hereafter).

        Despite the advantages of the FVM (e.g., simplicity and ease of practical implementation), the performance of FVM can be limited in practice for at least two considerations. First, when there are multiple cameras involved and the calibration images are taken on a static target, the calibration images captured on some of the cameras may be of low quality and therefore impair the accuracy of the VR. For example, when a camera captures the calibration image of the target from a very shallow angle, this calibration image is severely out of focus and only a small portion of it provides usable information. Or as another example, when the calibration image captured by a camera on a certain angle is near a singularity point of the VR algorithm, this calibration image contributes negligible information to the VR while causing instability [7]. Second, when there are restrictions on optical access such that the calibration image cannot be obtained with optimal orientation or quality (e.g., only a partial image of the target is captured), the accuracy of VR will also suffer.

        With the above understanding, this work therefore develops a new VR procedure, code-named the variable view method (VVM), to overcome the limitations of the FVM discussed above. The fundamental concept of the VVM is to rely on calibration images taken from a target at various orientations and/or locations, instead of only from a fixed view and location as in FVM. Due to the availability of multiple calibration images obtained at various views, the VVM can overcome the limitations of the FVM aforementioned. First, the quality of the calibration images on each camera can be guaranteed even where multiple cameras are involved. Second, in the presence of restrictions on optical access such that the calibration image cannot be obtained with optimal orientation or quality (e.g., only a partial image of the target is captured), the additional information provided by multiple views compensates the non-optimal orientation and incomplete image and enhances the VR accuracy.

        With the above understanding, this work first developed the mathematical formulation to describe the above VVM idea, coded the formulation to materialize it, performed controlled experiments to demonstrate and compared VVM and FVM methods, and lastly studied the potential impact of the new VVM method on laser imaging measurements.

The imaging process projects a target in the 3D world system through a camera (placed in a 3D camera coordinate system, C-XcYcZc, as shown in Figure 1) into a 2D image (placed in a 2D coordinate system, P-XpYp as shown). Figure 1 illustrates these three coordinates involved in the VR process. Without losing generality, this work assumes the target to be a flat calibration plate overlapping the O-XY plane. The origin of system O-XYZ (i.e., point O) is located at the center of this calibration plate. The position and orientation of the plate in system O-XYZ are known. The origin of system C-XcYcZc (i.e., point C) is at the optical center of the camera lens with the ZC axis directing towards the calibration plate. The image plane P-XpYp is parallel to C-XY (not necessarily parallel to O-XY) and its origin P lies at the center of the camera chip on the negative ZC. Using the pinhole model, the distance between P-XpYp and C-XcYc equals to the focal length f of the camera lens. The origins of all three coordinate systems are aligned on the optical axis of the camera.

        The traditional VR approach is to use 1 view (i.e., N=1). As mentioned above, this view may be of low quality and suboptimal due to optical access or field of view restrictions. Mathematically, these issues would cause large uncertainties in the elements of the J matrix in Eq. (7), and/or cause the J matrix to have a large condition number and become numerically unstable.

        The essence of the VVM method described in this work involving using calibration images captured from various views and/or locations to improve the VR accuracy. This work only explored the variation in orientation and from here on will only refer to orientation variation. Mathematically, improvements can be obtained at least for two reasons. First, varying the relative orientation of the views and camera mean the extrinsic parameters T⃗\vec{T}​T​⃗​​ and om⃗\vec{om}​om​⃗​​ can be freely chosen to avoid poor-quality views, and the accuracy of the elements in J (which are functions of and ) as shown in Eq. (7) can be maximized. Second, increasing number of views provides more constraints on the unknowns, leading to increasingly overly-determined system and enhanced insensitivity to noises. More specifically, each additional view introduces 6 more extrinsic unknown parameters (3 in om⃗\vec{om}​om​⃗​​ and 3 in T⃗\vec{T}​T​⃗​​) [7], but produces 8 more constraints at the same time. As a result, each additional view adds 2 more constraints in net on the unknowns.

III.Experimental Arrangement

        The goal of our experiment is to study the applicability of VVM in a controlled setup. Figure 1 illustrates the schematic of experimental setup. The experiment arrangements included three major components: a calibration plate (Calib Optics CG-150-O-9), a rotation stage (Thorlabs CR1-Z7), and a CMOS camera. The calibration plate had a chessboard pattern with black and white squares (dimension of each square is 9mm by 9mm, with an accuracy of 1.5 m) evenly placed on the board. The rotation stage was able to rotate continuously with a resolution of 0.0165 degrees in the whole 360 degrees range. The calibration plate was mounted on the rotation stage as shown, so that the relative position of the plate can be altered accurately by turning the stage. The CMOS camera (Cannon EOS 70D) was placed

at a distance of 44 centimeters away from the calibration plate. The camera was equipped with a macro lens (Micro-

        To better describe the experiments and the VVM method, three angles (θi, βi, and \gamma) were defined as shown in figure 1. First, θi represented the ith rotation angle of the calibration plate, directly read from rotation stage of the ith turn. The second angle, βi, represented the angle between the Z axis and the Zc axis, i.e., the angle between the optical axis and the normal direction of the calibration plate. The third angle, \gamma, represented the angle difference between the corresponding β and θ, i.e., the azimuth angle of the camera relative to the reference angular position. As an particular example to illustrate the meaning of these angles, if for the ith view, the optical axis of the camera was perfectly perpendicular to the calibration plate, then θi = βi, and \gamma= θi - βi =0. Here, we use \gammaVVM, \gammaFVM, and \gammaFVM to represented the \gamma obtained from the VVM method, the FVM method, and the experiments, respectively.

        With the above description of the experimental setup and notations, the experiment and data analysis process can be explained in four steps. The first step involved adjusting the calibration plate so that the plate was aligned approximately perpendicular to the camera. After the initial approximate alignment, we used the number of pixels in each black and white squared on the image (see Figure 3a for an example of such image) to fine tune the alignment. More specifically, for each image taken at a given position, the number of pixels in each black and white squares was counted, and then the alignment was adjusted until the pixels in each black and white squares were equal within an acceptable range. In the second step, the calibration plate was slowly rotated for 6 times, 15 degrees each time. At each rotation location, the angle θi (i=1 to 6) and the image of the calibration plate was recorded. Third, with the VR method programmed according to Section II, the angle βi for all of the 6 positions was calculated. In the fourth and final step, γ was calculated as θi - βi and then was used to quantified the error of the FVM and VVM method (i.e., due to experimental errors caused during the observation and numerical errors in the program). With these processes mentioned above, the γ was calculated for both the VVM and FVM method, and compared against the measured results to analyze the performance of the VVM method.

Figure 3(a) shows the image taken when the calibration plate was in its initial position of taken at θ=0 degrees, and Figure 3(b) shows the image taken when the calibration plate was at θ=60 degrees. As can be seen, when the calibration plate gradually rotated and faced the camera at increasing slanted orientation, increasing portion of the image became blurred and the edge between the white and black square became increasingly vague. Due to such blurring feature observed from figure 3a and 3b, the number of useful points that can be accurately extracted from images taken at different orientations varied from view to view. To quantify
such blurring, this work defined a parameter, \delta, the distance on the image to transition across the edge from a white square into a black one on the calibration plate. We took 10 pictures of the calibration plate at each rotation position,
and calculated the mean of \delta for better accuracy. The results are plotted in Figure 3c. As seen, as the calibration plate
gradually rotated to increasingly more slanted angles relative to the camera, \delta increased monotonically. Under ideal conditions (geometric imaging formation and perfect optics), when the calibration plate was at the initial 0 degree
position, \delta should be zero (i.e., less one pixel) in the image as the precision of the target was finer than the pixel size of the camera. However, as can be seen, \delta wasn’t zero at the initial position due to diffraction limited image formation and other non- idea features in the images system. Also note that at different f#, the degree of blurring was different. The remainder of the work was performed at a f# of 3.5. At this f#, \delta increased from 4.6 pixels to 5.1 pixels within the

IV.Results and Discussion

        Using the experimental set up and the procedure described above, view registration were performed both by the FVM and the VM method. Both FVM and VVM results (\gammaVVM and \gammaFVM) were compared to the measured value (\gammamea) to quantify the accuracy of each method. Figure 4 summarizes the qualitative comparison of VVM and FVM at various relative orientations between the camera and the calibration target. Figure 4a compares the angular positions determined by the FVM and VVM method (γVVM and γFVM) against the measured values (γmea) read from the rotation stage. As seen, they all overlapped closely with each other. To better illustrate the difference of VVM and FVM, Figure 4(b) shows the VR error of γVVM and γFVM (defined as the difference of the FVM and VVM results compared to γmea). As can be seen, VVM achieved reduced VR errors than FVM for 6 out of 7 camera positions. And the averaged VR error for the VVM method was 0.40°, while for FVM was 0.78°, a ~48% reduction.

Figure 4. Panel (a): calculated angles of cameras by VVM and FVM compared with benchmark measurements.Panel (b): VR error for VVM and FVM.

        After the above validation experiments with the calibration plate, here we demonstrate the application of a VVM method in tomographic PIV measurements. In this demonstration, controlled velocity fields generated by a solid sample were used to quantify the accuracy of tomographic PIV measurements, so that the error obtained using the FVM and VVM method can be compared quantitatively. The controlled velocity fields were generated using the experimental setup shown in Figure 5A. The key concept of this setup is to mount a solid sample seeded with particles

5

on a stage to generate the target velocity field. By controlling the movement of the solid sample through the stage precisely (e.g., with 10 µm and 5 arcmin accuracy for translational and rotational movement, respectively, in this work), the particles seeded in the sample generate well-controlled velocity field with precision well beyond the accuracy of the velocimetry technique, and hence can be used as the ground truth to directly quantify measurements uncertainties. As shown in Figure 5A, the solid sample were assembled with four thin glasses with a thickness of 0.5 mm each, height of 65 mm, and width of 60 mm. The glasses were stacked together with a gap of 0.55 mm in between two adjacent ones. Each glass was coated with polystyrene particles of average diameter of 4.05 m on one side. The solid sample was then mounted on a rotational and translation stage that can rotate and move continuously with 5 arcmin and 10 m precision. The output beam of PIV laser was expanded to illuminate the particles volumetrically so their positions can be seen and recorded by a number of cameras. A total of 6 cameras were placed around the stage (with two shown in Figure 5A for illustrative purposes) to capture the movement of the illuminated polystyrene particles while turning the stage. The images recorded by all of the six cameras during the movement were used as inputs for a three-dimensional tomographic PIV reconstruction. Then, the reconstructed velocity field was compared with the ground truth to quantify the error. Using this setup, a variety of velocity fields can be generated by a combination of translational, rotational, and tilting movements to simulate practical issues in controlled ways. Figure 5B shows an example of a velocity field generated by rotating the sample by 2.50 degrees.

        In this work, the tomographic PIV reconstruction was performed using the measured projections twice: once using the VR results provided by the traditional FVM method and the second time using those provided by the VVM method. The reconstructions were then compared against the known rotational field as shown in Figure 5B (the ground truth) to quantify the error, and the results are summarized in Figure 6. The results are presented in terms of the cumulative percentage of the error of the magnitude of the velocity. More specifically, the error of the velocity magnitude was calculated voxel by voxel in the reconstruction domain. Figure 6 then shows the cumulative percentage of such error (the vertical axis) larger than a certain level (the horizontal

6

axis). For example, the two data points on the far right of Figure 6 means: when VR was processed by the VVM method, error larger than six voxels occurred on less than 1% of all the voxels; and when it was processed by the FVM method, the error larger than six voxels occurred on nearly 10% of all the voxels. The improved VR accuracy brought about by the VVM method was therefore able to reduce PIV error larger than 6 voxels by 11x. As another example, error larger than 5 voxels occurred on about 2.4% of all the voxels when the VVM method was used, and about 17% of all the voxels when the FVM method was, resulting in a 7x reduction in the PIV error due to the improved VR accuracy provided by the VVM method. Similar results have been obtained with other types of velocity fields generated by the controller sample. These controlled experiments demonstrated that the VVM method can significantly enhance the overall accuracy of three-dimensional PIV measurements, and furthermore also reduce the large errors. Considering that in many practical applications, the derivatives of velocity is of ultimate importance rather than the velocity field itself. Since the derivative operation tends to magnify measurement uncertainties, the benefits brought about by the VVM method to significantly reduce both overall and large errors is expected to be valuable in practice.

V.Summary

        This work developed a new view registration method—VVM for optical measurements. Compared to the conventional fixed view method (FVM) using a limited number of stationary views, VVM adopted a large set of views to both enhance the quality of points selected and also to better constrain the problem. The performance of VVM was examined both numerically and experimentally and compared to the FVM, demonstrating superior accuracy. The implication of such superior performances in tomographic PIV is studied and discussed.
